    Here is a list of things a friend of mine who is way more versed in protesting than I and will likely be joining me Sunday sent last night. She is clearly opinionated but I feel like everything here makes sense on some level. I hope this is helpful to anyone.

    —quote—

    I hope you wont need this:

    Put three tsp Milk of Magnesia in 16 ounces of water, splash on skin or eyeballs to relieve chemicals intrusions. Always guide chemicals FROM inner TO outer corner of eye, AWAY from tear duct. Never guide anything toward your tear duct. The pain from a chemical burn is excruciating, but it is temporary.

    If someone calls you from a rooftop, don’t look up. They do this so that they can get a clear photo of your face…

    Make sure to wear lots of non-oily sunscreen, rubbed in WELL, and bring way more water than you think you need. Drink in small sips frequently, try to avoid making yourself have to pee. I recommend sunglasses you don’t care about and a hat with a brim, but your goal is to look as nondescript as possible, so nothing too fancy. “Bucket” style hats are good. Do NOT wear a mask, this is grounds for arrest. Pack your hipsack with a bandanna to hold in front of your nose and mouth if they gas you, the bandanna is folded, soaked in vinegar, sealed in two ziplock bags. Also packets of emergen-C, band-aids, apples and Larabars, bring extras to give away if you like. People will want them. Hair is tied together and up or back, shoes are the most comfortable ones you’ve ever had. Your hands are free, your cellphone is fully charged. Your lawyer’s phone number and an emergency number are written on your arm in Sharpie, but under your shirt where they are not immediately visible. Your clothes are the type that will keep you dignified in jail when stripped down to their lowest common denominator: t-shirts and simple pants are good, nothing too complicated that jail guards might take issue with. If you get arrested, they will take any extraneous layers, ie: You can’t wear more than one shirt, so no button-fronts over t-shirts, or anything like that.

    You do NOT follow the crowd, that’s how you get penned in. Marching is fun, but always know an escape route. You are always aware of the crowds’ movements, you don’t follow them “off a cliff.”

    Practice what to do if you get arrested. Breath deeply and slowly, talk in a friendly way with your fellow protesters, but don’t tell them anything in case they’re undercover. Do not tell the police ANYTHING. You CANNOT talk your way out of getting arrested or charged, do not say ANYTHING other than your name, address, date of birth. Say NOTHING except “I want to talk with my attorney.” If you are nice to jail guards, they will be nice to you, but do not tell them ANYTHING, no matter how harmless it seems. THERE IS NOTHING YOU CAN SAY TO THEM THAT WILL HELP. I cannot emphasize this enough. Busting protesters is a video-game to cops. They feel that they are “against” you. It doesn’t matter how smart or charming you are, they want to take you down. Jail guards are typically nicer than cops, but not always.

    If you get arrested, do NOT freak out. Remember that this too shall pass, no matter what happens, you will soon be safe in your own bed. Even if it takes a few days, you WILL get out eventually, and you will NOT be in any serious trouble. There will be lots of fellow protesters with you, and they will help protect you if you let them. Align with them immediately. The point of jail is to confuse you. A confused prisoner is an obedient prisoner. Be polite in jail no matter what, and try to keep a smile on your face to help the time go by. Remember, this is TEMPORARY. Singing in jail is fun if your friend are into it, but don’t get on their nerves. No matter how frightening things get in jail, you are NOT in serious trouble, and you WILL get out soon. No matter how much they try to confuse or scare you, it just doesn’t matter. It’s temporary, stay brave, it will be over soon no matter what.

    If you are alert enough, you can typically avoid arrest, but cops do lunge over barriers to accost pretty girls, people they think look vulnerable, etc. If a cop grabs someone near you, either get the fuck away, or grab the person and try to pull them back to your side. This is a split-second judgment call, and can be very difficult. Obviously, let go rather than hurting the person. For this reason, I recommend marching in the center of the block, not near the rails. If you’re near the rails, you’re on high alert for grabby hands.

    You will probably not get arrested, so don’t let this freak you out too much. Be prepared to witness abysmal behavior on the part of the authorities, but don’t let it crush you emotionally. Be brave, you are doing the right thing. Do not let them scare you. When you live in terror, terrorists win. Be vibrant. Have so much fun that the cops want to join your side.

    Occupy DC Protest October 1, 2011 â€“ Day One – President Obama Drives By!!!

    I arrived at McPherson Square just after 12 noon to take part in the Occupy DC protest. I’m standing in solidarity with Occupy Wall Street and all the other nationwide protests beginning to take place. I never thought We’d actually see President Barack Obama, but we did! More on that in a little bit…

    It was a very chilly 50-55 degrees with light rain showers off and which likely dampened the turnout of protesters and activists. But several dozen diehard folks refused to let bad weather suppress their voices of discontent. At the peak of the turnout my best guess would be that there was 50 to 75 people. I spent the first of couple hours speaking with protesters on K street (K Street in NW DC is the epicenter of lobbying firms, and just two blocks from the White House) who were holding up signs, waving, and interacting with traffic. They were getting lots of honks from passing cars, and positive feedback from pedestrians. You can listen to all of those interviews and audio from the protest on the FFR audio page.

    Most of the protesters, probably 75 % on them, were in their 20′s-30′s. Some were carrying signs saying â€œHonk if you have student debt,â€ which most of the people that I spoke with did in fact have.

    The overall atmosphere was very positive, respectful, and courteous. Everyone was cooperative with all laws. There were no police to be found for a majority of the day. Until a little after 5pm when we began to see a few police cars stationed at all four corners of McPherson Square, the site of the protest. I wasn’t paying close attention to the time as I was speaking with other fellow activists, but it was just beginning to get dark, my best guess is around 7pm. When all of a sudden we began to hear police sirens. Lots of sirens. Within a matter of seconds, several dozen police cars, all with lights flashing, had the entire park surrounded. I knew something was up.

    I was looking around trying to see why all these police suddenly showed up out of nowhere. There was no sign of a traffic accident, no sign of an emergency, and no reason for tons of police. Then I began to start to see motorcycle police, one after another, in single file coming up 15thSt NW crossing I St and heading towards K St. I had this deep gut feeling something was about to happen. I grabbed one of the signs from our pile, it read â€œCorporations Are Not People,â€ and began running to the corner of 15th and I St, while shouting to all of the activists to grab a sign because something was happening.

    Police cars lined all the surrounding streets, several traffic police on foot were standing in the intersections. The nearest officer on foot noticed all of us bunching up on the sidewalk and came towards us, blowing his whistle and demanding that we all step back from the street. We all obeyed the orders. Then, at that exact moment, we realized from all the black SUV’s with tinted windows, that it was the Presidential motorcade. One protester shouted, â€œIt’s Obama! Obama is here!â€ Literally right on cue there it was: President Obama’s new armor-plated Cadillac Limousine known to insiders as â€œBeast.â€ And for the first time in my life, I saw the President of the United States with my own eyes.

    I was speechless, almost in a mild shock. There he was. The man we’d been trying to reach with our message. Everything was moving in slow motion for me. In real time he was there for no more than two or three seconds. I saw him turn and look at us. I just stared, others around me began to cheer enthusiastically. It was a truly amazing moment. Unfortunately, I didn’t think to turn my audio recorder back on until the motorcade was already out of view.

    We all chatted about it for the next hour, with a strong sense of joy and excitement. We all gathered near the south end of the park under some large trees to help shield the rain. An hour later, as we were talking and sharing feelings about the President driving by, the motorcade made another pass. This time going in the opposite direction, heading back towards the White House, down the very same one-way street.

    This time I continued to sit under the trees, not rushing to the street like most of the other protesters who were still there did. It was a very satisfying feeling to know that we might have actually brought some direct awareness to the leader of the free world. To me it really felt like we had a voice, and our voice was heard. Even if it was only for a few passing seconds.

    It was quite surreal for me to see the President, to be standing a mere 20 feet from him, even if it was just for a second. The White House was just two blocks away and he drove past us en route to and from his speech at the Human Rights Campaign Dinner tonight. Hell, he might not have even known about the protest. But I know for a fact that he saw us and we saw him. This is just the start of citizens making their voices heard. In time, he will know who we are and what we are trying to do. But for now, I’m satisfied in knowing that his motorcade drove past our protest.
